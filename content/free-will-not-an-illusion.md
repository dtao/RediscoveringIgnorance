---
title: Free Will is Not an Illusion
date: 2026-04-13
---

- I tend to believe that abstractions are real things. When I look at
  a rock, or a tree, or a cloud, there is something really there; it is
  not "merely" a collection of atoms. The atoms are arranged as they
  are because they are part of something larger, which we are able to
  recognize, and which we've given a name.
- At the same time, I believe in the connectedness of all things. So
  when I see a tree, I might wonder "Where does the tree end and the
  rest of the world begin?" Leaves that are a part of the tree fall to
  the ground, and then they're not. The roots stretch into the ground
  and absorb nutrients. If I zoom in closely enough, the boundary may
  begin to blur.
- Many people seem to struggle with blurry boundaries. We want things to
  be neat and tidy. Something is either right or wrong, true or false.
  The bark of the tree is part of it, the ants crawling along the bark
  are not part of it.
- I like to point to day and night: when precisely does "day" become
  "night"? It often happens without me even noticing. And yet none of
  us would claim that day and night are illusions; they are real, and
  for much of any 24-hour period, in most parts of the world, it is
  obvious. At noon it is unambiguously day. At midnight it is
  unambiguously night.
- The same applies to all people, including the self. The more I
  reflect, the blurrier the boundary between "me" and "the rest of the
  world" seems to get: I am shaped not only by genetics, but by my
  upbringing, my environment, my relationship with everyone around me.
  I am not merely my thoughts, or my body, or just an observer, or just
  an actor in the world. I seem to be all of these things. I am
  connected, but I am real.
- When we talk about "free will", I believe it's easy to become
  confused. People will suggest that we live in a deterministic
  universe (which may be true), or that our conscious awareness of our
  actions follows our decisions (which may also be true), and then
  conclude that "free will is an illusion", that it doesn't exist. But
  to me this viewpoint loses contact with any coherent definition of
  free will.
- Here is my own coherent definition. I think that "will" refers to the
  choices I make and the intentions behind them. And I think that "free"
  refers to the idea that I am not a mere billiard ball bouncing around
  the world on a path that's entirely dictated by external forces.
- A friend of mine once said to me that we don't have free will because
  _what we want_ is determined, i.e. we don't get to pick what we
  actually want. Whether or not this is true, from my perspective it's
  completely beside the point of whether we _have_ will (I believe we
  do) and whether we're free to exercise it (I believe we are).
- The other most common argument seems to be that we are not actually
  "free" because our choices are ultimately determined by a huge number
  of factors. But if you ask someone to list what all of those factors
  are, they will inevitably include some things, like your DNA and your
  upbringing, that I believe _are a part of you_. To me this would be
  like me saying, "I didn't commit the crime, your honor---it was
  actually my hands that did it!" Again, whether or not these things
  are deterministic does not really matter; the answer to that question
  merely reveals whether or not _I_ am deterministic (and if I'm not,
  then some of my choices are presumably random, which isn't how I
  typically think about being free).
- One last common point I hear, supposedly in support of the idea that
  free will is an illusion, is that there are studies that show we
  often make decisions before we're consciously aware of them. Once
  again, I generally view this as a red herring that only seems
  compelling on first glance because it is a bit surprising, which does
  not in fact directly contradict any coherent definition of free will.
  I am not sure why conscious awareness would be a prerequisite for it.
  If you were to ask someone making this claim, "So if people are not
  consciously aware of their decisions, then what factors are leading
  to those decisions?" I believe the response you would get is that
  people's _unconscious_ minds are making decisions, or that they're
  acting purely on instinct, or that we're all preprogrammed to behave
  the way I do, or some combination of those things. What all of those
  possible explanations have in common is that they refer to things
  that, once again, are actually part of what makes you who you are.
- So we come back to the abstraction of the self: while we are connected
  to everything else, and while the lines between us and the world may
  blur as we examine them (often in a way that I personally find
  comforting), we still exist, and we do things, and it is really us
  who do them.
