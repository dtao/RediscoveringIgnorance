---
title: Don't "underpromise", just commit and deliver
date: 2026-04-17
---

- Conventional wisdom: "underpromise, overdeliver"
- As a one-off delighter, this can work great. As a system, it's bad
- When you make a habit of underpromising, you unconsciously create a
  safety net for yourself - one that you will depend on, maybe not
  always, but sometimes
- This leads to a pattern of only actually trying to deliver the modest
  result you promised, occasionally exceeding it if you're lucky
- More realistically, too often that "modest" promise somehow turns into
  a stretch goal... and then you don't even clear that
  (telling yourself, "they'll understand, sometimes things slip")
- A track record of consistently hitting modest goals is not something
  that really sets you apart - you become the "safe" choice
  (hopefully - see above) but not the go-to for any serious
  challenges - but a track record of *not even hitting modest goals* is
  even worse.
- Imagine if we applied this in other areas: marriage ("I'll do my best
  to stay with you for at least a few years"); professional sports
  ("I'll get us at least a few wins this season"); startups
  ("we'll raise a couple rounds, then we'll probably have to close
  shop")
- What if you actually asked yourself "How much can I achieve if I truly
  do my best?" and committed to that? How much more would you deliver
  over the course of a year? A career? A lifetime?
- This is not just about "work really hard at your job"; it's about
  applying yourself in every aspect of your life and not hiding behind
  an imaginary disappointment shield. You may think it's protecting
  you - and it might be, but it might also be holding you back
- Stop under-promising. Commit and deliver.
