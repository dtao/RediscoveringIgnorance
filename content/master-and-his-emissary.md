Months after finishing Iain McGilchrist's *The Master and His Emissary*, I still catch myself narrating the world through it. A stranger reads a situation and insists on finding who's to blame: classic left hemisphere. Someone else hears the same story and starts asking how everyone got swept into it together: classic right hemisphere. I'll say the words out loud sometimes, half-joking, mostly because I can't stop seeing the dichotomy everywhere I look. A book hasn't done that to me in a long time.

McGilchrist's claim, stripped to its bones, is that the two halves of the brain attend to the world in fundamentally different ways---and that one of them is meant to be in charge. The right hemisphere takes in the whole: context, relationship, the sense of a thing as continuous with everything around it. The left hemisphere specializes in isolating, naming, breaking down. The title gives you the thesis. The right is the master, the left the emissary. The emissary is brilliant and useful; he is also not the one who should be running the kingdom.

McGilchrist thinks he has been, for centuries now. Not forever---the book walks through long swings through history, from the ancient Greeks to the Renaissance to the Enlightenment, pointing to periods when one hemisphere's way of attending held more cultural weight than the other. But modernity, he argues, has tilted hard toward the left. Industrialization rewards standardization and reduction. Capitalism rewards abstraction. Algorithms, more recently, reward legibility of the kind only the left hemisphere cares about. Each force pushes the same direction: away from the whole, toward the part.

The part of the book I found most haunting is the description of how the two hemispheres are supposed to relate. The right takes in everything at once and delegates to the left for focused analysis. The left does its work and hands what it has found back. The right synthesizes the result into a richer understanding than it had before. Each side is necessary; the coordination is what matters. What McGilchrist worries about is a modern failure mode in which the handoff back never happens---the emissary learns to operate on his own, forgets the master exists, and starts issuing decrees in his name.

I should clarify, because this is easy to misread. McGilchrist is not anti-analysis. He is not saying the left hemisphere is bad, or that rigor and precision and categorization are to be abandoned. He says the opposite: those faculties are extraordinary and the world we have is unimaginable without them. His claim is about relationship, not value. The left needs to be subordinate to the right because the left, left alone, is overconfident. It doesn't doubt. It doesn't see what it has missed. It is an extremely capable employee who is convinced he should be CEO.

That ring of truth is what kept the book in my head for months. I see left-hemisphere-gone-rogue everywhere once I start looking. In the compulsion to find a single person to blame when something goes wrong, as if causality could be pinned on one node in the graph---when in reality almost every bad outcome has a dozen hands in it, and the question worth asking is not *whose fault was it* but *how did we collectively end up here, and how do we avoid it next time*. In the reduction of human beings to labels---Republican, Democrat, this race, that religion---as a way of skipping the actual person in front of you. In the confidence with which strangers on the internet declare what is true, untroubled by any sense of what they might be missing. Each of these is, in McGilchrist's terms, the emissary making decisions the master should be making.

I am not qualified to evaluate the neuroscience. McGilchrist clearly is, and the book is thick with studies and patient cases and cited experiments, and I mostly took him at his word. But even if the mapping onto actual brain anatomy turned out to be wobblier than he claims, the underlying distinction---between the mode of attention that takes in the whole and the mode that breaks it down---would still be the most useful framing I've encountered for what goes wrong in a lot of modern thinking.

And yet. I was describing all of this to a friend who lives in India, and his reaction stopped me. He saw the prescription as the last thing his culture needs. He lives, as he described it, surrounded by astrology, superstition, and intuition elevated above evidence, and he longs for more of the rational, scientific, left-hemisphere mode---not less. Meanwhile in America I see what looks to me like the opposite pattern: every woman I know seems to know her astrological sign now, where a decade ago it would have been a slightly embarrassing admission. I can read that shift, through McGilchrist's lens, as a healthy correction to an overly reductive culture. My friend would read the same phenomenon in his own country and call it regression.

That doesn't refute the book. But it does suggest the prescription depends on where you are starting from. McGilchrist's diagnosis---the emissary has usurped the master---may be the right diagnosis for a hyper-rational, data-driven, algorithmically-mediated modern West. It may be exactly wrong for a culture that already privileges intuition and inheritance over analysis. The master-and-emissary relationship is probably a real and important thing. Which one currently needs to be reined in is local.

Most lenses seem to me to be like this. They illuminate something real, which is why they resonate, but their prescriptions are rarely universal. *The Master and His Emissary* has left a deep impression on how I see, and I suspect it will for a long time. The natural next move, for me, is to look for a lens that's nothing like it---something with an equally vivid claim about how to attend to the world, pointed in a completely different direction. Other books I'm considering reading after this one are *The Art of Thinking Clearly* by Rolf Dobelli, *Ishmael* by Daniel Quinn, and *The Sacred and the Profane* by Mircea Eliade. I don't expect any of them to dethrone McGilchrist's framing. But I'd like to see what it looks like through different eyes.
